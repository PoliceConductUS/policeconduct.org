## Design Context

### Users

- Primary audience: members of the public who have had an interaction with police and need to document it, understand it, or place it in a broader public record.
- Secondary audience: partner organizations represented under `src/pages/partner`, including groups that research police behavior, support accountability work, or collaborate with the project.
- Users may arrive under stress, with anger, distrust, or urgency. The interface must help them act without adding confusion or spectacle.

### Brand Personality

- Credible
- Courageous
- Objective

The interface should feel public-interest, trustworthy, and accountability-oriented. It should acknowledge that some visitors are arriving because they believe they experienced injustice, while still presenting information with discipline and factual restraint.

### Aesthetic Direction

- Civic and editorial rather than startup-like or marketing-heavy.
- Serious, clear, and durable. The site should feel like a public-interest record, not a campaign landing page or police industry publication.
- Light mode remains the primary presentation.
- Dual-theme support is acceptable, but dark mode must stay sober and restrained. Avoid neon accents, glowing effects, purple-blue gradients, glassmorphism, or anything that looks trendy, aggressive, or synthetic.
- Partial references: [Institute for Justice](https://ij.org/) and [National Registry of Exonerations](https://exonerationregistry.org/) for public-interest credibility and research orientation.
- Anti-references: [CUAPB](https://www.cuapb.org/) and [Officer.com](https://www.officer.com/).

### Design Principles

- Lead with trust. Users must quickly feel that the site is credible, careful, and built to preserve records responsibly.
- Keep moral seriousness without melodrama. The interface should allow for outrage at misconduct without becoming sensational or theatrical.
- Make action paths obvious. Reporting an interaction, researching local police, and finding partner-specific entry points should all be easy to spot.
- Favor objective presentation over decorative flourish. Typography, spacing, and structure should do most of the work.
- Support both calm research and urgent reporting. The system should serve users who want to investigate deeply and users who need to submit something immediately.
- If dark mode is implemented, it should feel institutional and readable, not futuristic or entertainment-oriented.
