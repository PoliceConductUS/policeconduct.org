## Design Context

### Users

- Primary audience: members of the public who have had an interaction with
  police and need to document it, understand it, or place it in a broader public
  record.
- Secondary audience: defense attorneys, journalists, advocates, civic
  researchers, and partner organizations researching police behavior or
  accountability work.
- Users may arrive stressed, skeptical, angry, distrustful, urgent, or
  time-constrained. The interface must help them act and research without adding
  confusion, spectacle, or implementation detail.

### Brand Personality

- Credible
- Courageous
- Objective

The interface should feel sober, civic, public-interest oriented, trustworthy,
and accountability-oriented. It should acknowledge that some visitors believe
they experienced injustice while still presenting information with discipline,
factual restraint, care, and evidence-backed transparency. Positive findings are
allowed only when evidence-backed and clearly scoped.

### Aesthetic Direction

Use a light-mode-first civic/editorial product style: restrained navy text, thin
rules, compact tables, measured spacing, muted teal and deep red accents where
useful, and clear hierarchy. Prefer dense but scannable research surfaces over
marketing layouts. Civic index pages should feel like practical public-records
tools, not landing pages.

Typography must be consistent across the site. Use one primary sans family for
product/data pages, including the home page, so H1/H2/H3 roles do not drift by
template. Editorial credibility should come from hierarchy, spacing, rules,
content structure, and restraint, not from switching font families page by page.

Avoid gradients, glassmorphism, decorative blobs, sensational language, dark
neon dashboards, nested cards, generic AI metric walls, and placeholder copy
that exposes internal data collection gaps. When data is not available, show an
empty measurement or neutral placeholder such as `$--` rather than language that
announces missing data.

### Design Principles

- Make the primary next action obvious. For geography pages, the default action
  is usually drilling into child entities: counties/areas, places, or agencies.
- Lead with trust. Users must quickly feel that the site is credible, careful,
  and built to preserve records responsibly.
- Keep moral seriousness without melodrama. The interface can support
  accountability work without becoming sensational or theatrical.
- Keep route and data identity visible through user-facing paths and labels, but
  never expose implementation mechanics or collection caveats as product copy.
- Prioritize residents and legal researchers: fast local lookup, credible record
  summaries, searchable lists, and clear paths to personnel and civil cases.
- Use charts only when they answer a real civic question. Empty graphs are
  acceptable as neutral measurement space, but they should not overwhelm the
  page or imply false precision.
- Preserve shared navigation, breadcrumbs, and database-backed URL conventions
  across all civic index pages.
- Follow `DESIGN.md` for typography, spacing, color roles, component vocabulary,
  and heading tokens before changing frontend code.
