# capture-policeconduct-data-infrastructure

Capture recent PoliceConduct.org data-source, national crawl/index, TPIA, policy-taxonomy, dataset-import, evidence-graph, transparency-index, and local-agency report-card work as implementation specs.

This change set now includes a public-facing local agency report-card concept inspired by official law-enforcement standards reporting, but inverted toward visitor needs: transparency, accountability, force and enforcement, governance and cost, staffing, training, policy quality, and year-over-year change.

It also adds a national data-source crawl/index backlog. The long-term data goal is to paint a comprehensive, provenance-backed picture of every officer and agency in the country where lawful public data supports it: pay, staffing, arrests, stops, searches, use of force, complaints, commendations, discipline, training, questionable training events, public-records behavior, funding, asset forfeiture, custody, bail, dispositions, suppressed evidence, reversals, juvenile interactions, in-custody injury or death, and arrest-to-final-disposition traceability.

The transparency score is expanded into a fine-grained agency transparency index. The index should show not only whether an agency is generally transparent, but exactly which records are published, whether the data is machine-readable, how complete and fresh it is, and how predictably the agency responds to public-records requests.

It also makes positive deviance a first-class data-infrastructure goal. PoliceConduct.org should help identify officers, supervisors, units, agencies, policies, and practices that produce unusually good public-trust and public-safety outcomes under comparable constraints. This positive-first model is similar in spirit to Accelerate-style research in software delivery: study high performers, understand what they do differently, and translate evidence-backed patterns into capabilities others can evaluate and adopt.

It adds a court-claim review task inspired by Joanna C. Schwartz's _Civil Rights Without Representation_, 64 Wm. & Mary L. Rev. 641 (2023): locate police-related civil-rights claims, especially pro se and procedurally dismissed matters, where court records show indicators of potentially legitimate claims without overstating unproven allegations.
