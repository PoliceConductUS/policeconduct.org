# Monell Audit 

For Monell, the audit should cover the **entire criminal-process pipeline**, not just jail intake. The question is not merely “did one officer do something wrong?” It is:

> **Did the combined municipal system — police, supervisors, jail, prosecutors, city attorneys, policymakers, and courts-facing staff — operate through a policy, custom, or practice that predictably caused the constitutional injury?**

Under *Monell*, the municipal entity is not liable just because it employed the wrongdoer; the injury must be tied to an official policy, custom, practice, final-policymaker decision, ratification, or training/supervision failure that was the “moving force” behind the violation. ([Justia Law][1])

So yes: the audit should cover **every group, agency, department, and person involved from first contact through final disposition**.

## The Complete Monell Pipeline Should Include

### 1. Pre-Arrest / Street Enforcement

This includes dispatch, patrol officers, supervisors, task-force officers, and any pre-stop intelligence systems.

The audit should examine:

* retaliatory stop or arrest;
* viewpoint-based enforcement;
* filming-the-police retaliation;
* refusal-to-consent retaliation;
* selective enforcement;
* false or exaggerated probable cause;
* supervisor approval of escalation;
* bodycam muting or nonactivation;
* unlawful phone seizure;
* unlawful search;
* use of obstruction, disorderly conduct, trespass, resisting, or failure-to-obey charges as flexible punishment tools.

Pattern evidence would include prior similar arrests, dismissals, complaints, bodycam, suppression rulings, and officer/unit histories.

---

### 2. Police Report / Evidence / Probable-Cause Paperwork

This stage is often where the constitutional problem gets laundered into “official” form.

The audit should examine:

* boilerplate probable-cause affidavits;
* omission of protected conduct;
* omission of exculpatory facts;
* false “interference” language;
* report-writing templates;
* supervisor approval of defective reports;
* bodycam contradictions;
* post-hoc justification;
* supplemental reports that repair defects after the fact.

Pattern evidence would include prior reports by the same officer/unit, RMS metadata, report drafts, supervisor edits, and prosecutor feedback.

---

### 3. Jail Intake / Booking / Detention

This is its own major Monell stage.

The audit should examine:

* threats of restraints for invoking silence;
* coercive intake questioning;
* punishment for refusing nonessential questions;
* denial or delay of phone access;
* “not booked yet” used as a delay tactic;
* delay in booking to prevent calls, counsel, bond, or magistrate access;
* unequal classification;
* unnecessary isolation;
* restraint threats;
* ignored medical needs;
* failure to preserve intake video;
* refusal to document complaints;
* delay or prevention of attendance at the first available magistrate hearing.

For warrantless arrests, *Gerstein* requires a prompt judicial determination of probable cause before extended restraint of liberty. ([Justia Law][2]) *County of Riverside v. McLaughlin* generally treats probable-cause determinations within 48 hours as presumptively prompt, but delays within 48 hours can still be unconstitutional if they are for improper purposes, such as gathering more evidence, delay for delay’s sake, or other unreasonable administrative reasons; delays beyond 48 hours shift the burden to the government to justify them with a bona fide emergency or extraordinary circumstance. ([Justia Law][3])

So the question is not only “was it under 48 hours?” The better question is:

> **Was the person taken to the first reasonably available lawful process, or did the system use booking, classification, paperwork, phone restrictions, or transport timing to delay access to court, counsel, release, or complaint-making?**

---

### 4. Magistrate / Initial Appearance Access

This stage is critical because municipalities sometimes hide delay inside “administrative processing.”

The audit should examine:

* who controlled transport or video appearance;
* whether the detainee missed the first available docket;
* whether similarly situated detainees appeared earlier;
* whether paperwork was intentionally delayed;
* whether the jail blamed police, police blamed jail, and prosecutors blamed paperwork;
* whether “not booked yet” prevented appearance;
* whether no one had responsibility for ensuring timely presentment.

Pattern evidence would include docket rosters, transport logs, booking completion timestamps, probable-cause affidavit timestamps, jail release logs, court appearance logs, and comparator detainees.

---

### 5. Prosecutor / City Attorney Screening

This stage asks whether the prosecuting authority acted as a meaningful constitutional checkpoint or merely rubber-stamped defective arrests.

The audit should examine:

* filing charges based on protected speech;
* filing obstruction/disorderly charges without element review;
* continuing charges after bodycam contradicted probable cause;
* failing to reject retaliatory arrests;
* failing to disclose officer credibility issues;
* using charges to pressure plea or release conditions;
* dismissing cases quietly without notifying police leadership of recurring defects;
* maintaining municipal ordinances known to be overbroad or misused.

Pattern evidence would include charging guidelines, declination data, dismissal data, prosecutor notes, city attorney files, municipal court outcomes, and repeated cases involving the same officers or charges.

---

### 6. Pretrial Conditions / Bond / Release

The audit should also examine whether the municipal system used process itself as punishment.

Look for:

* unusual bond positions;
* no-contact orders used to suppress criticism;
* stay-away orders from public property;
* conditions barring recording or public comment;
* delayed release after bond posted;
* unequal treatment compared with similarly situated defendants;
* hold policies;
* refusal to process release paperwork promptly.

Pattern evidence includes bond logs, release timestamps, jail communications, prosecutor bond recommendations, and comparator case outcomes.

---

### 7. Discovery / Evidence Disclosure

This stage can support a due-process or municipal disclosure-system theory.

The audit should examine:

* failure to disclose bodycam;
* delayed disclosure until after plea pressure;
* missing or overwritten jail video;
* failure to disclose officer complaints;
* failure to disclose Brady/Giglio material;
* failure to disclose prosecutor declination patterns;
* no system for police-to-prosecutor credibility disclosure;
* no system for jail video preservation.

Pattern evidence includes discovery logs, Brady/Giglio policies, evidence-retention policies, prosecutor-police communication, IA disclosure protocols, and prior late-disclosure incidents.

---

### 8. Plea / Dismissal / Final Disposition

Final disposition matters because repeated dismissals can prove that the system keeps charging weak or unconstitutional cases.

The audit should examine:

* charges repeatedly dismissed after defendants incur jail, bond, lawyer fees, or court appearances;
* dismissals used to avoid rulings on constitutionality;
* plea offers conditioned on waiving complaints;
* diversion used for speech-based arrests;
* city attorney dismissing without correcting the underlying practice;
* repeated “no probable cause” or “insufficient evidence” dispositions involving the same charge type.

Pattern evidence includes dismissal orders, plea offers, prosecutor notes, municipal court data, and settlement history.

---

### 9. Complaint / Oversight / Discipline / Ratification

This is often the strongest Monell notice layer.

The audit should examine:

* complaints ignored or closed despite video;
* no discipline after unlawful arrests;
* no retraining after dismissed cases;
* IA findings that contradict video;
* city attorney or police chief public defense of the conduct;
* settlement without policy change;
* repeated lawsuits with no corrective action;
* policymakers praising or ratifying the challenged conduct.

Pattern evidence includes IA files, complaint summaries, settlement approvals, council minutes, chief statements, risk-management records, and training revisions or lack of revisions.

---

## The Better Audit Structure

The complete audit should be organized by **process stage**, not just by legal theory:

| Stage                | Actors                                     | Core Question                                                            |
| -------------------- | ------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| First contact / stop | Officer, dispatch, supervisors             | Was enforcement triggered by protected conduct or unsupported suspicion? |
| Arrest decision      | Officer, supervisor                        | Was the charge used as punishment or pretext?                            |
| Report writing       | Officer, supervisor                        | Was probable cause manufactured, exaggerated, or sanitized?              |
| Evidence handling    | Police, records, prosecutors               | Was exculpatory evidence preserved and disclosed?                        |
| Jail intake          | Jail staff, police, supervisors            | Was booking used to punish, coerce, delay, or isolate?                   |
| Phone/counsel access | Jail staff                                 | Was access delayed longer than legitimate administrative need?           |
| Magistrate access    | Jail, police, court transport, prosecutors | Did the detainee reach the first available lawful review?                |
| Charging             | Prosecutor/city attorney                   | Were weak or retaliatory charges screened or rubber-stamped?             |
| Pretrial conditions  | Prosecutor, court-facing staff, jail       | Was process used as punishment?                                          |
| Discovery            | Prosecutor, police, jail                   | Were videos, reports, complaints, and credibility evidence disclosed?    |
| Disposition          | Prosecutor/city attorney                   | Do outcomes reveal a pattern of weak or retaliatory cases?               |
| Oversight            | IA, chief, city attorney, council          | Did policymakers know and fail to correct?                               |

## Best Master Framing

Use this as the guiding statement:

> **The Monell inquiry should examine the entire municipal criminal-process pipeline, from first contact through final disposition, to determine whether the plaintiff’s injury was caused by an isolated act or by an interlocking policy, custom, practice, failure, or ratification across police, jail, prosecution, city attorney, and policymaker functions.**

And the best pleading theory is:

> **The plaintiff’s injury was not complete at arrest. It was produced and prolonged by a municipal pipeline: retaliatory enforcement, defective probable-cause paperwork, coercive or delayed booking, denial of timely phone/court access, rubber-stamped charging, delayed disclosure, and failure to correct known recurring misconduct.**
