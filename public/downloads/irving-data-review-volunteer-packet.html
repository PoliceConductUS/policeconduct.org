Irving Public Records Data Review Volunteer Packet

Thank you for offering to help. This project is using public records to understand how the Irving Police Department handles arrests, officer assignments, complaints, uses of force, jail intake, public-records requests, and related accountability records. Irving is the launch agency, but the long-term goal is to build a repeatable public-records and data-review model that can be used for every law-enforcement agency in the United States.

The goal is not to hunt for one predetermined violation. We are looking for patterns in the data, including patterns that may show misconduct, weak supervision, inconsistent enforcement, unusual case outcomes, missing records, or records-handling problems. We are also looking for the opposite: exemplar officers whose histories show effective policing, restraint, accurate reporting, low complaint rates, and good outcomes.

What we are trying to learn

The project is built around a simple question:

What does Irving's own data show about policing practices, accountability, and records transparency?

The larger question is whether the same method can be applied agency-by-agency, so residents, journalists, lawyers, researchers, and advocates can compare what departments say their policies require against what their records show officers and supervisors actually do.

We are collecting records across multiple categories so the data can be compared. A single spreadsheet may not say much by itself. The clues usually appear when records are connected across systems, dates, officers, outcomes, complaints, use-of-force records, and public-records correspondence.

Analysis lens

We are interested in the kinds of factors that matter when civil-rights investigators, journalists, lawyers, and community groups evaluate whether a police department has a pattern or practice problem. That includes not just the arrest itself, but the full context around the encounter and the department's response afterward.

Useful clues may include:

  • the officer's beat, unit, shift, assignment, and supervisor;
  • the specific neighborhood, location, call type, and reason officers were there;
  • race, ethnicity, age, gender, housing status, disability, or other available demographic data for the person stopped, arrested, searched, or subjected to force;
  • the person's apparent attitude or demeanor as described by officers, witnesses, video, and records;
  • whether the officer used discretion to warn, cite, release, arrest, search, escalate, call backup, use force, or refer to services;
  • whether similar people in similar circumstances were treated differently;
  • whether the written policy appears to be followed in practice or functions mostly as lip service;
  • whether supervisors identify, correct, tolerate, or ratify repeated problems;
  • whether complaints and uses of force are investigated seriously or closed in predictable ways;
  • whether officer reports match video, dispatch, jail, medical, court, and outcome records;
  • whether some officers consistently show effective policing, restraint, good judgment, accurate reporting, and strong outcomes without generating unnecessary complaints or force.

This is also why exemplar officers matter. If some officers handle the same beats, neighborhoods, call types, and difficult encounters with fewer complaints, less force, better documentation, and stronger case outcomes, their records can show what effective policing looks like inside the same department.

Volunteers can also suggest their own conduct hypotheses and the data requests needed to support or falsify them. A strong hypothesis should be testable. For example: "Officers on one shift use one charge more often than comparable officers," "complaint outcomes differ by unit," or "some officers get better case outcomes with less force." The point is not to prove the hypothesis. The point is to ask what records would confirm it, weaken it, or show that a different explanation is more likely.

Upcoming records and data requests

These are the main categories of records we expect to receive or continue requesting.

1. All arrests, by year

This is all arrest data, not only public-intoxication arrests. Public intoxication may be one category we analyze later, but the request is broader because we do not want to assume the pattern in advance.

Possible review questions:

  • Which officers make the most arrests?
  • Do some officers have unusually high arrest rates compared with peers in similar assignments?
  • Are certain charges used unusually often by particular officers or units?
  • Are there spikes by year, shift, location, event type, or officer?
  • How often do arrests lead to dismissal, no-file, deferred disposition, conviction, or other outcomes?
  • Are there officers with high arrest volume but unusually weak case outcomes?
  • Are there officers with moderate arrest volume and strong case outcomes, low complaint rates, and low force rates?

2. Arrest-linked event, call, and encounter context data

Arrest records are more useful when connected to the underlying call or encounter.

Possible review questions:

  • What types of calls lead to arrest?
  • Which arrests began as consensual encounters, welfare checks, traffic stops, disturbance calls, or officer-initiated activity?
  • Are there patterns in backup calls or escalation?
  • Are some officers more likely to convert low-level encounters into arrests?

3. Officer roster, assignment, and employment records

We need officer identity and assignment records so arrests can be compared fairly.

Possible review questions:

  • Which officers were active during each year?
  • What units, shifts, or assignments were they in?
  • Did arrest patterns change after reassignment, promotion, discipline, or training?
  • Are comparisons being made between officers with similar roles, not apples to oranges?

4. Complaints, use-of-force records, and arrest-outcome comparator records

These records help connect conduct allegations, force, and case outcomes.

Possible review questions:

  • Which officers have repeated complaints or force incidents?
  • Do complaint histories correlate with arrest outcomes?
  • Are complaints sustained, closed without investigation, or classified in ways that hide patterns?
  • Are there officers with low complaint and force rates who still maintain effective enforcement outcomes?

5. Jail intake, medical, vital-sign, and custody records

These records may show how arrestees are handled after arrest.

Possible review questions:

  • Are medical screening and vital-sign practices consistent?
  • Are intoxication, injury, force, or medical-risk indicators recorded consistently?
  • Are there gaps between arrest reports, booking records, and jail records?

6. Policies, training, and supervision records

Policies and training records help explain what should have happened.

Possible review questions:

  • What policies governed arrest decisions, backup requests, use of force, complaints, and jail intake?
  • Were officers trained on those policies?
  • Do patterns in the data suggest policy failure, training failure, supervision failure, or individual outliers?

7. Public-records correspondence and Attorney General letters

The records process itself is part of the project. We are tracking requests, correspondence, AG letters, productions, delays, missing fields, and format changes.

Possible review questions:

  • Did the City produce records promptly?
  • Did it release public or non-exempt information while waiting for AG rulings?
  • Did it produce usable data or flattened PDFs?
  • Were fields omitted without explanation?
  • Were similar requests handled consistently over time?

What volunteers can help with

There are several ways to help, depending on what you enjoy and how much time you have.

Request drafting and submission support

Help identify the records we need, draft focused public-information requests, submit requests when appropriate, and keep the request language clear enough that an agency can respond without guessing.

Volunteers can also suggest their own conduct hypotheses and the records needed to support or falsify them.

Request tracking and compliance review

Track pending requests from submission through final production. That includes:

  • logging submission dates, tracking numbers, and deadlines;
  • watching response times;
  • noting Attorney General letters, cost estimates, clarification requests, and productions;
  • checking whether the agency releases public, basic, non-exempt, and segregable information promptly;
  • checking whether the agency treats requestors consistently;
  • flagging delay, missing fields, unexplained redactions, format problems, or blanket withholding.

For Irving, we already have several pending requests that volunteers can help track.

First-pass document and production review

Read productions, correspondence, AG letters, spreadsheets, PDFs, and request drafts. Flag anything that seems important, inconsistent, missing, or confusing.

Data cleanup and analysis

Help identify columns, field meanings, duplicate records, inconsistent naming, missing dates, missing officers, and other cleanup issues. Then help compare the records across systems: arrests, calls, officers, assignments, complaints, force, jail records, court outcomes, and public-records correspondence.

Pattern spotting

Look for things that repeat. Examples:

  • same officer appearing in unusual clusters;
  • same type of charge appearing after similar encounters;
  • missing fields in one production but not another;
  • cases with force but no complaint record;
  • complaints with no outcome;
  • arrests with weak or missing disposition data;
  • officers who appear to resolve situations effectively without generating complaints or force records.

Public presentation

Help think through how to publish the data so it is useful to the public. That may mean plain-language summaries, searchable tables, charts, downloadable datasets, agency scorecards, issue pages, or short notes explaining what a dataset does and does not show.

The goal is not just to collect records. The goal is to make public records usable.

What we are not doing

  • We are not assuming every officer is bad.
  • We are not targeting one charge category as the whole story.
  • We are not treating raw accusation data as proof.
  • We are not publishing private information just because it appears in a production.
  • We are not asking volunteers to give legal advice.

The strongest work will be careful, boring, and well-documented. If a pattern is real, the records should show it. If an officer's record shows effective policing, that matters too.

Tools and workflow

We will use Google Drive as the main workspace for:

  • request drafts;
  • submitted requests;
  • City correspondence;
  • Attorney General letters;
  • productions;
  • spreadsheets and extracted data;
  • review notes;
  • issue trackers and summaries.

Helpful experience:

  • Google Drive folders and shared documents;
  • Google Docs comments and suggested edits;
  • Google Sheets filtering, sorting, and basic formulas;
  • careful file naming;
  • writing short research notes with citations to source files.

Pending requests and request tracking

We already have several pending public-information requests that volunteers can help track. That work includes watching request folders, logging City responses, noting Attorney General letters, identifying deadlines, comparing productions against request language, and flagging gaps.

Volunteers may also propose new requests. A good proposed request should connect to a clear conduct hypothesis and should identify what data would support, weaken, or falsify that hypothesis.

Burden, costs, and refresh cycle

We do not want to impose an undue burden on any agency. The long-term model should ask for records in a way that is focused, repeatable, and realistic for agencies to produce.

We also expect core datasets to be refreshed at least annually. The goal is not a one-time document dump. The goal is a public, maintainable record that can show trends over time.

No volunteer is expected to pay out of pocket for records. If a request results in a cost estimate, that request will be handed back to IPC for internal handling before anything is approved or paid.

Suggested first assignment

A good first task is to review one request folder and create a short note answering:

  1. What was requested?
  2. What did the City produce or say?
  3. What is missing or unclear?
  4. Are there deadlines or AG letters tied to this request?
  5. What follow-up question should we ask next?

A useful note can be one page. It does not need to be fancy. It should cite file names and page numbers when possible.

Confidentiality and care

Most of this work involves public records, but the records may still contain personal information, medical references, addresses, phone numbers, or sensitive allegations. Please do not repost, forward, or publish documents outside the project workspace without permission.

When in doubt, flag the issue and ask.
