# Positive-Deviance Operating Plan

PoliceConduct.org is not only a misconduct catalogue. The site should help residents answer: what would a resident need to know to judge whether this department, and each person working there, is safe, accountable, transparent, and improving?

That requires identifying harm, risk, opacity, and weak accountability. It also requires identifying positive deviance: people, teams, agencies, policies, practices, and community workflows that produce unusually good outcomes while facing comparable or harder conditions.

## Source Framework

This plan adapts the Positive Deviance Collaborative's framing:

- Positive deviants are individuals, groups, or entities that succeed despite facing the same or worse constraints as peers.
- The approach is asset-based, problem-solving, and community-driven.
- The method is commonly summarized as five D's: define the problem, determine whether positive deviants exist, discover uncommon successful behaviors and strategies, develop activities based on those findings, and discern results through monitoring and evaluation.
- Guiding principles include stakeholder ownership, social proof from peers, network-driven adoption, focus on practice, and collective monitoring.

## Positive-First Model

The Positive-First Model is similar in spirit to the research behind Accelerate in software delivery. Rather than studying only failures, outages, or defects, Accelerate identified high-performing organizations, measured what they did differently, and translated those patterns into capabilities other organizations could adopt.

PoliceConduct.org should apply the same logic to public safety and public trust. Identify officers, supervisors, units, and agencies that consistently produce better outcomes under comparable conditions, then study and publish the behaviors, policies, training patterns, supervision practices, disclosure habits, and community routines that may explain those outcomes.

Positive-first does not mean ignoring harm. It means pairing accountability analysis with evidence-backed discovery of practices that residents, agencies, journalists, researchers, attorneys, and policymakers can examine and potentially scale.

## Operating Workflow

### 1. Define

For each analysis domain, define the problem, common practices, constraints, and desired outcome before looking for positive deviants.

Examples:

- Problem: frequent use of force during mental-health calls.
- Desired outcome: fewer force events, fewer injuries, faster connection to care, and credible public documentation.
- Comparable constraints: call volume, staffing, poverty, housing instability, behavioral-health resources, service area, and dispatch model.

### 2. Determine

Determine whether positive deviants appear to exist among comparable peers.

Candidate signals:

- Lower force rates after adjusting for calls, arrests, and service population.
- Lower complaint rates with credible complaint access and non-suppressed intake.
- Faster, more complete public-records responses.
- Better training completion and less repeated discipline.
- Fewer dismissed charges or suppression issues tied to officer conduct.
- High clearance or problem-resolution metrics without disproportionate enforcement or rights costs.
- Strong outcomes in high-risk contexts such as crisis response, domestic violence, protests, youth encounters, and jail intake.

### 3. Discover

Discover uncommon but successful behaviors and strategies through data, records, observation, interviews where appropriate, and community review.

Potential discovery questions:

- What does this officer, supervisor, unit, or agency do differently from comparable peers?
- Is the difference a behavior, policy, staffing model, dispatch practice, training practice, supervision routine, transparency habit, or community partnership?
- Does the practice appear repeatedly, or only once?
- Is the result explained by underreporting, selection bias, lower enforcement activity, data suppression, or easier operating conditions?
- Do residents, complainants, defense attorneys, journalists, local advocates, and agency records tell the same story?

### 4. Develop

Turn discoveries into useful site artifacts and workflows:

- Agency report-card sections: "what appears to be working unusually well?"
- Officer and unit profile indicators for documented positive practices.
- Request templates that ask for data capable of revealing good practice, not only discipline.
- Video and source tagging for positive conduct when identifiable officers and source provenance are strong.
- Case studies that describe transferable practices without treating a single good outcome as a full endorsement.
- Data-model fields for positive-deviance hypotheses, comparison groups, source records, confidence, and review status.

### 5. Discern

Monitor whether positive-deviance findings hold up over time.

Each positive-deviance finding should have:

- a comparison group,
- date range,
- outcome metric,
- source records,
- known limitations,
- confidence level,
- date last reviewed,
- revision history,
- and a path for public correction or challenge.

## Data And Request Implications

Public-records requests should seek data that reveals both harm and better practice:

- officer rosters, assignment history, supervision, units, and tenure;
- training requirements and completions;
- complaints, findings, discipline, appeals, and reversals;
- use-of-force, stops, searches, arrests, warnings, citations, dispositions, and dismissals;
- crisis response, co-responder, diversion, and referral outcomes;
- public-records response logs and publication practices;
- commendations, awards, letters of appreciation, community feedback, and supervisor notes where public;
- policy changes, audit findings, accreditation materials, and corrective-action records;
- staffing, turnover, separations, and lateral movement;
- budget, grant, vendor, overtime, and settlement data.

The goal is not to create a praise file. The goal is to find comparable actors achieving better resident-facing outcomes and preserve enough context to understand whether their practices are credible, transferable, and durable.

## Publication Guardrails

- Do not label an officer, unit, or agency a positive deviant without a defined problem, comparison group, and source-backed outcome.
- Do not confuse absence of complaints with positive performance unless complaint access, intake, and transparency are also evaluated.
- Do not treat a single commendation, viral video, or agency press release as sufficient evidence.
- Do not publish sensitive personal data, victim data, medical data, or private complainant data to support a positive finding.
- Do not let positive-deviance findings erase documented harms. Profiles should support mixed evidence.
- Use cautious language: "candidate positive-deviance practice," "appears unusually strong on this metric," or "requires further review" when confidence is limited.
- Preserve provenance so visitors can inspect how the conclusion was reached.
