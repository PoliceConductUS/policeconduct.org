# Branding Specification

**Brand Intent**

PoliceConduct.org is a public-interest platform for documenting, understanding, and improving law-enforcement conduct.

The brand should not feel anti-police, pro-police, activist-first, or institution-first. It should feel civic, serious, fair, and human. Its role is to help communities see the full record of policing as it is experienced in real life: harm, trust, confusion, relief, misconduct, professionalism, positive deviance, improvement, and everything in between.

**Core Brand Promise**

PoliceConduct.org helps people document law-enforcement interactions with equal rigor, whether the experience was harmful, positive, mixed, uncertain, routine, or unresolved.

**Primary Message**

Every firsthand experience with law enforcement is legitimate and informative. A subjective experience is not a final judgment, but it is part of the public record of how policing is experienced.

**Positioning Statement**

PoliceConduct.org documents the full record of law-enforcement conduct: what happened, how it was experienced, what patterns emerge, what caused harm, what built trust, and what evidence shows agencies are improving or not.

**Core Doctrine**

Recognition and accountability are not opposites.

Communities need both:

- a way to document harm, fear, disrespect, delay, confusion, misconduct, or institutional failure
- a way to recognize professionalism, restraint, compassion, de-escalation, fairness, and unusually effective practice
- a way to understand everything in between

**Subjective Experience Principle**

Police complaints are often dismissed because the person “misunderstood,” “generalized,” or was “just upset.” PoliceConduct.org rejects that dismissal.

Being personally affected does not make someone’s perspective invalid. If someone felt unsafe, protected, unheard, respected, intimidated, helped, confused, or harmed, that experience matters.

The platform should validate the experience without overstating it as a proven finding.

**Required Distinctions**

PoliceConduct.org should clearly distinguish between:

- what happened
- what the person directly observed
- how the person experienced it
- what the person believes it meant
- what records, evidence, policy, or other accounts may support, clarify, or complicate it

This allows the site to honor subjective experience while remaining fair and evidence-aware.

**Tone**

The brand voice should be:

- civic
- calm
- serious
- human
- evidence-aware
- bridge-building
- trauma-aware
- non-performative
- careful with claims
- clear about harm

It should not sound:

- adversarial by default
- defensive of institutions
- dismissive of complaints
- falsely neutral about harm
- promotional toward police
- casually activist
- legalistic to the point of coldness

**Core Language**

Use language like:

> Your experience is legitimate, even when others may see the interaction differently.

> Not every troubling interaction is misconduct. Not every positive interaction proves an agency is healthy. But every firsthand experience can help communities understand how policing works in real life.

> We do not dismiss experiences because they are subjective. Policing is experienced by people, and those experiences are part of the record.

> PoliceConduct.org documents harm, positive deviance, and everything in between with equal rigor.

> The goal is not to choose sides. The goal is to preserve the record, reveal patterns, support accountability, and identify what actually builds trust.

**Avoid**

Avoid language that implies:

- complaints are noise
- balance means minimizing harm
- positive stories offset misconduct
- all experiences are equal in severity
- the platform decides guilt from one report
- officer reassurance matters more than resident trust
- bridge-building requires softening accountability

Avoid phrases like:

> not a complaint department

unless paired with a stronger accountability promise.

**Better Alternative**

> PoliceConduct.org is a civic record for recognition, concern, accountability, and learning.

**Audience Outcomes**

A harmed resident should feel:

- my experience counts
- I do not need to prove misconduct for this to matter
- the site will not exaggerate my account
- the site will not dismiss my account
- my perspective can help reveal patterns

A fair-minded officer should feel:

- good work can be recognized
- context matters
- the process is not designed to attack me
- claims are handled carefully
- professionalism and improvement are visible

An agency should feel:

- this record may reveal concerns
- this record may reveal strengths
- public trust requires both
- defensive dismissal is not the goal
- learning and accountability are possible

A researcher or journalist should feel:

- the data is structured
- subjective experience is preserved responsibly
- claims are distinguished from findings
- patterns can be studied
- positive deviance is part of the evidence base

**Success Criteria**

The branding succeeds if visitors understand within the first page that PoliceConduct.org is:

- about conduct, not just misconduct
- about lived experience, not just formal complaints
- about patterns, not isolated outrage
- about accountability without hostility
- about recognition without endorsement
- about bridge-building without minimizing harm

The core test:

> Does this page make clear that every firsthand experience matters, and that PoliceConduct.org documents it with care, context, and equal rigor?
